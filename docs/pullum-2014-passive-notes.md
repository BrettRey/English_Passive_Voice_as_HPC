## Core claims

Pullum argues that popular advice against the English passive is both grammatically confused and stylistically overstated. He gives a broad descriptive account of passive constructions, then documents repeated cases where critics label non-passive clauses as passive.

The paper matters because it treats the passive as a heterogeneous constructional domain rather than a single simple template. It also stresses that many textbook definitions are too narrow to describe the actual English facts.

## Points likely useful for this paper

1. Passive is not reducible to a single surface formula such as `be + past participle`.
2. English has multiple passive-like constructions, including short passives, prepositional passives, bare passives, embedded passives, get passives, adjectival passives, and concealed passives.
3. Complaints about passive often conflate grammatical voice with rhetorical evasion of agency.
4. Information structure matters. Pullum argues that long passives are constrained by discourse status, especially the relative newness of the by-phrase.
5. The article provides strong evidence that folk and style-guide categories around passive voice are unstable and often internally inconsistent.

## Relevance to an HPC framing

This article does not propose an HPC analysis, but it is a strong background source for one. It supports the idea that the English passive is not best modeled as a category with a short necessary-and-sufficient feature list.

More specifically, Pullum's survey suggests a cluster structure:

- some cases have `be`
- some have `get`
- some have a by-phrase
- some omit the by-phrase
- some are finite clauses
- some are bare or embedded
- some are closer to adjectival predication

That makes the paper useful as descriptive groundwork for arguing that passive voice in English behaves more like an HPC category than like a sharply bounded classical category.

## Caution for drafting

Pullum's main target is bad usage advice, not category theory. The cleanest use in this project is probably:

1. first, cite Pullum to establish the descriptive complexity and widespread misanalysis of passive constructions;
2. second, pivot to the claim that HPC modeling explains why the category remains recognizable despite lacking a single invariant checklist.
